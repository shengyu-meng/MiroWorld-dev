## Design Context

### Users
MiroWorld is for public-audience viewers, artists, curators, researchers, and workshop participants who enter a speculative worldline and watch how events, rules, materials, environments, institutions, natural forces, and human actors bend one another. They may not write any input at all; the interface must still carry them through a progressive, consequential experience.

### Brand Personality
Cosmic, precise, consequential. The product should feel like an exhibition instrument: quiet enough to invite looking, strange enough to feel authored, and rigorous enough that every visual cue appears tied to a real world-state or process artifact.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction is an orbital theatre: dark starfield, small black-hole anchor, amber accretion energy, thin cyan/violet telemetry lines, sparse floating panels, and visible process traces. The local `.ui-ref` package is a reference for atmosphere and interaction language only; do not copy or commit it. The interface should not look like a report dashboard, enterprise analytics tool, social-media opinion simulator, or generic glass-card AI layout.

### Design Principles
- Lead with the worldline, not the report. The stage, orbit, and revealed nodes are primary; text panels are instruments around the phenomenon.
- Every choice shows a cost. Branches, process layers, interventions, and archive outputs should expose pressure, confidence, and afterimage rather than presenting a single score.
- Progressive disclosure beats density. Viewers can keep pressing Next, while deeper process files, calibration, archive, and intervention affordances appear at meaningful points.
- Non-human actants matter. Language and layout should support rules, materials, natural objects, environments, constraints, and institutions as first-class participants.
- Performance is part of the artwork. Motion should feel alive but stay light: precomputed canvas paths, reduced-motion fallback, adaptive density, and no live LLM dependency for ordinary navigation.
